CN Women Gender

Our industry of choice is policy makers and community advocates. Our findings and products can assist the government bodies, global and local organizations to collectively erase gender inequality in China, especially by providing more education opportunities for girls and women.


Impact and Goals

Education is a human right. When it comes to gender equality, universal and fair access to educational opportunities should be a key area of concern. Our project aims to highlight differences in educational opportunities and resources across different regions in China. How do gender ratio and time as well as regions correlate with educational opportunities of Chinese girls and women? Disparity in educational resources start from primary education and extend all the way to higher education. While our study will primarily focus on educational attainment level, we attempt to uncover the bigger picture of how education is essential while evaluating and improving gender equality across China.

The United Nation’s Education 2030 Agenda points out that government bodies hold the main responsibility for ensuring the right to quality education, and at the same time it is a universal and collective commitment. Our project will provide useful insights for policy makers and contribute to the progress towards a more equal future for the education of girls and women in China.

An Average of 2.19% Difference between male and female population


The population % = the # of population of a gender / the total population of that year

From 1998 to 2020, female population percentage (purple line) was constantly lower than male population percentage (green line), suggesting an uneven gender distribution over the entire country.

As of 2006, China's gender population gap was at its minimum. The population percentage difference between male and female was 1.08%.

However, the gap grows wider and wider after that year.

As of 2021, the gap becomes 2.34%, which almost doubled during the 15 years.

Gender Ratio by Regions

The gender ratio = the # of male population / the # of female population

Over the course of 22 years, the gender ratio decreased for coastal regions, while increased for underdeveloped regions like Tibet and Xinjiang.

Even for some major cities like Shanghai and Beijing, the gender ratio dropped drastically.

However, there are also some provinces experienced the opposite. Jilin province's gender ratio changed from 1.04 to 0.997 during the 22 years.

Sadly, the overall gender ratio gets worsening during these years, which suggests a sign of inequality.

Mean yearly illiterate ratios from 1999 to 2021

The illiterate ratios = the # of illiterate population of a gender / the # of population of the gender above 15 years old

From 1999 to 2021, female illiterate ratios (purple line) were constantly higher than male illiterate ratios (green line), suggesting unequal access to educational resources between genders. Females had less access to elementary education than males.

Good news is that both female and male illiterate ratios have been decreasing, and the gender gap (orange bars) has been reducing as well. Although till 2021 females were still having a larger illiterate ratio than males, the gender gap is as small as 3.9%. At least for the access to elementary education, the inequality between genders is decreasing.

Mean regional gender gap of illiterate ratios

Overall, the whole country has demonstrated reduced gender gaps, as shown by the lighter orange colours from 1999 to 2021.

Some regions started with relatively larger gender gaps, but the gaps have been lowered to less than 10% since 2012. Some of the regions were in the inner land, such as Gansu, Qinghai and Guizhou, which were less strong in economies. Some of the regions were on the eastern coast, such as Shandong, Jiangsu, and Fujian, which had strong economies. Only one region remained in a gender gap larger than 15% until 2021, which is Tibet.

It seems that the inequality between genders was not solely dependent on geographical locations or economical power.

All Educational Levels

We have collected “currently enrolled students” stats from 1999 to 2015 in the PRC and compared the student numbers in both men and women in each educational category: Higher Education, Secondary Education and Primary Education.

The number of male and female students have both been dropping in recent years, this is likely due to the one-child policy and the decreased birth date overall. In the Primary Education category, it has been dropping at least since the year of 1999. In the Secondary Education category, we see the downward trend of both men and women starting around 2005 and 2006.

However, in terms of higher education, the overall student enrollment has consistently increased in the past 20 years. It is also interesting to see that in 2009, the number of enrolled female students exceeded the number of enrolled male students. It did not slow down from there, the gender gap continues to increase yearly after 2009.

Unfortunately, stats regarding enrolled student gender are no longer available in the published data set by the Chinese National Bureau of Statistics, which is the reason why our analysis in this project cannot go beyond the year 2015 yet.

Higher Educational Levels

As the overall student enrollment has consistently increased in the past 20 year, we know that this is not a phenomenon in China alone, in fact, it is part of an international movement where in recent years, women have become the majority on college campuses in many countries around the globe. At the same time, while diving deeper into the data, we discover that while there were more women overall enrolled in college in China, there is an uneven distribution of the gap - the highest differences are reflected in the undergraduate departments, while the traditional gender gap very much remains in the postgraduates field at the highest educational level (Postgraduates - Doctor’s Degree).

Population and Students Gender Ratio

The large gender gap might have surprised you in the [All Educational Levels] diagram, however, keep in mind that China has a large population gender gap to begin with. In this diagram, we focused on comparing the population gender ratio from 2010 with the enrolled students gender ratio at each educational level. The population gender ratio is estimated according to the 2010 census.

For the primary education category, the population gender ratio represents the gender ratio in children aged from 6 to 12. For the secondary education category, age 12 to 18. For the higher education category, age 18 to 27. The gender ratio difference is much smaller in the primary education level. It is at the secondary education level we start to see the significant difference. While the gender ratio for the overall population of this age group is only at 1.0673, the educational gender gap is at 1.4390, which means for every girl that’s enrolled in secondary education, 1.44 boys are enrolled. This shows that the opportunity of education is very uneven between the two genders, this puts girls at a significant disadvantage early in life.
